1. Voyage of the Walrus

The Walrus is a robust bluewater sailboat designed and built by T.C. Ces Watson in 1946 at his Tutukaka design studios. Constructed from old-growth New Zealand kauri using triple-planked traditional techniques and bronze fastenings, it was built to endure New Zealand’s aggressive ocean conditions. Over the decades, it crossed the Pacific Ocean three times, traveling between New Zealand, California, and beyond, proving its seaworthiness and the quality of its construction.

2. Builder’s Standard

T.C. Ces Watson spared no expense in the construction of The Walrus. Using the highest quality kauri timber available, the boat features heavy-duty triple planking, extensive bronze fasteners, and a design that prioritizes strength and durability. This vessel was built not just for function but as a testament to New Zealand’s premier boatbuilding craftsmanship, capable of withstanding the harshest ocean environments.

3. Service at Sea

The Walrus served as a personal sailing vessel throughout its life. Its numerous ocean crossings tested its build against open-water swells and unpredictable weather. Thanks to its robust design and superior materials, it remained structurally sound and reliable without frequent repairs, a testament to the durability of the kauri timber and craftsmanship.

4. Reclamation

After nearly 80 years of service, The Walrus, a heavily overbuilt vessel weighing approximately 34,000 pounds, was rigorously dismantled over two and a half months. Due to its robust triple-planked construction and extensive bronze fastenings, traditional salvage methods were impractical. Instead, an artisan woodsmith undertook the demanding process almost single-handedly, recovering significant old-growth kauri timbers and key components that retained much of their structural integrity and historical character.

5. The Artisan Woodsmith’s Journey

The reclamation of The Walrus culminated a twelve-year personal journey rooted in respect for history, craftsmanship, and nature. Approaching the work as an artisan woodsmith, the dismantling and salvage were acts of stewardship rather than commercial salvage, aiming to preserve the wood’s integrity and story, and acknowledging the deep connection between human creativity and natural legacy.

6. The Material’s Second Life

The reclaimed kauri timbers carry a profound history—both ecological and human—and now enter a new phase through creative transformation. Once part of ancient forests and integral to a vessel that braved the Pacific, these woods are shaped into jewelry, instruments, and art. Each piece embodies the wood’s unique qualities and layered stories, extending its journey in forms that can be held, worn, or played.

7. The Ecological Significance

Kauri trees (Agathis australis) are foundational species in northern New Zealand’s forests, able to thrive in nutrient-poor, waterlogged soils where few other species succeed. Their deep ecological role includes shaping and controlling their environment, supporting a unique ecosystem through symbiotic relationships that have evolved over millions of years since their ancestors originated in Gondwana roughly 240 million years ago. Kauri forests represent a living link to ancient times and are known for the unparalleled resonance of their timber, prized worldwide.

8. Cultural Reverence

The Māori hold kauri trees in deep spiritual regard, honoring their mana (prestige, authority) and mauri (life force). Since their arrival, Māori have maintained the sacredness of kauri, using its wood carefully for waka (canoes), carvings, and tools, always respecting the tree’s significance within the forest and community.

9. Colonial Impact

The arrival of Europeans led to widespread kauri logging driven by demand for shipbuilding and construction. From the late 18th century onward, vast forests were exploited with inefficient and often destructive methods such as damming and flooding forests, burning groves, and careless waste. The government sold huge tracts for minimal prices, accelerating deforestation and profoundly altering the landscape and Māori land ownership through land courts and colonization. The Musket Wars (early 19th century), including the tragic genocide of the pacifist Moriori people, further complicated this era’s social upheaval.

10. Legacy and Preservation

The timber used in The Walrus came from some of the last of the highest quality old-growth kauri available—trees that we will never see again. This wood, sourced by T.C. Ces Watson in post-WWII New Zealand, represents a vanished era of craftsmanship and natural grandeur. Today, as kauri forests face threats like disease and habitat loss, salvaged timber like this carries forward a legacy of resilience, story, and reverence.
